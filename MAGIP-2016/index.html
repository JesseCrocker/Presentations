US Topo - No There There
The USGS started from scratch making the new US Topo map series in 2009 as a replacement for the 7.5" quads. These maps are produced digitally, they have the classic USGS look, but there is so little information on on many maps that they are almost useless.
OpenStreetMap to the rescue!
You might be familiar with OpenStreetMap as that ugly map used by people too cheap to pay for google maps. But it's much more than that. It's a worldwide crowd-sourced open licensed database of geographic knowledge.
OpenStreetMap has over half a million contributors, some have only made 1 contribution, some map 50 hours a week. In many areas the map was not started from scratch, but was seeded with imports of public domain data.
In the US TIGER roads and NHD hydrology was imported, and starting from that base the community has built a map. It's not perfect, especially in rural areas, but in highly trafficed areas it's really good, and in almost all areas it's better than US Topo.
Not everything we need
But OpenStreetMap does not contain everything we need to make a topo map. It doesn't have contour lines.

A few notes about technology Tile Pyramid Image
The maps i'm going to be talking about are generated with vector tiles. Most maps you see on the web are tiled maps, but they are raster tiles.
tile-xray images.
Vector tiles contain geometries and attributes instead of images.
Why?
Vector tiles add an additional level of abstraction between the data is what the user sees, and that abstraction does have a cost, but the cost is outweighed by the benefits.
Multiple Styles.
Querying features from a database is generally the slowest part of rendering a map, and with vector tiles you can make those queries once to generate the vector tiles, and then render multiple map styles from the same vector tiles.
